📈 SaaS MARKET ANALYSIS - June 17, 2026

Market Overview
•The global SaaS market is estimated at $435.41 billion in 2026 and projected to reach $976.61 billion by 2031, implying 17.55% CAGR over 2026–2031.[1]
•A second 2026 estimate puts the market at $465.03 billion, with growth to $1.37 trillion by 2035 at 12.85% CAGR; the gap versus other forecasts reflects different methodologies and segment coverage.[2]
•Growth is being driven by enterprise cloud migration, rapid AI integration, and the economics of pay-as-you-go delivery.[1]
•In 2025, North America held the largest regional share at 42.60% in one major forecast, while Asia-Pacific is expected to be the fastest-growing region at 18.7% CAGR through 2031.[1]
•Another forecast places North America at 46% of the market in 2025 and also identifies it as the largest region, reinforcing its dominance despite forecast differences.[2]
•Within the market, large enterprises accounted for 58.05% of SaaS demand in 2025, while SMEs are the fastest-growing cohort at 19.2% CAGR.[1]
•By vertical, IT and Telecom led with 22.45% share in 2025, while Healthcare is the fastest-growing vertical at 22.9% CAGR.[1]

Investment & Funding Trends
•The strongest capital concentration is in AI-native SaaS, where multiple market trackers report accelerated spending and rapid product adoption; Zylo reports 108% year-over-year growth in spending on AI-native SaaS applications.[7]
•BetterCloud reports that 95% of companies have invested in AI-driven use cases, indicating that AI is now a mainstream budget line rather than a pilot category.[5]
•Funding is also flowing into agentic AI, with one 2026 trend report describing an $11.8 billion market in 2026, $57.4 billion by 2031, and a 143% YoY funding surge in Q1 2026.[8]
•SaaS monetization is shifting toward usage-based and consumption-based pricing; Zylo reports Gartner expects 70% of top SaaS vendors to offer some consumption-based pricing by 2027.[7]
•This pricing shift tends to favor categories with variable workloads and high AI inference costs, such as developer tools, observability, data platforms, and AI workflow software; that is an inference based on the pricing data and category economics reported in the sources.[7][8]
•The available sources do not provide a reliable June 2026 dataset for average valuation multiples by stage, so stage-specific multiples should be treated cautiously unless supplemented with current private-market comp data.
